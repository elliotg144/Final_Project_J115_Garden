Feeling Under the Weather

Rough Patch

After Tropical Storm Isaias came through the Eastern side of the United States, gardens throughout the area were severely displaced. These storms often come come quick leave blown down trees, split stalks, and scattered debris in their path. In order to repair, gardeners have introduced jute, stakes, and a whole lot of out-of-the-box thinking to bring things close to the way they were.

Tying it Together

Jute is a common type of twine used to reattach and and hold together split stalks. It's comprised of hemp fibers which can withstand high rates of stress while maintaining ultimate flexibility. A wide variety of knots are used to sinch together damaged plants.

Interview with Marty the Farmer

E: How many of these storms do you think you've been through since you've moved here?

M: I'd say probably around 5 real rough ones. I've learned to appreaciate them more now though, they tend to teach you something. I've definitely learned how to tie better knots and strategies when it comes to staking

E: Tell me about that, your tips and tricks.

M: Well, I first thing I did after my first storm was buy a post driver. I'd have to say it's one of my favorite tools up here, you just get to slam the crap out of each stake you place. Aside from that, I've incoporated trellaces into almost all portions of my gardens, whether I'm growing things that cling or not.

E: What would you say is your least favorite part of the storms that hit here?

M: My least favorite part would be coming out the next day and seeing the wreck. You cross your fingers but sometimes the winds are too much. It teaches you to be grateful though. At least I can go to the grocery store and pick up whatever I don't have, I couldn't imagine growing to eat, like back in earlier times
